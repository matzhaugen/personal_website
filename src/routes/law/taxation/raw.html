The constitutional argument

The constitutional argument which both support the above statement and the IRS code is as follows. Income tax is an excise or duty and as such amounts to an indirect tax not subject to apportionment. This follows from Article 1, Section 8, Clause 1 of the Constitution and F. Morse Hubbard, Treasury Department legislative draftsman, in Congressional testimony in 1943:

[T]he amendment made it possible to bring investment income within the scope of the general income-tax law, but did not change the character of the tax. It is still fundamentally an excise or duty...,

also "[T]axation on income [is] in its nature an excise, entitled to be enforced as such..." Brushaber v. Union Pacific R. Co., 240 U.S. 1 (1916),

and
”[I]ndirect taxes are the familiar federal taxes imposed on activities or transactions. That category of taxes includes duties, imposts, and excise taxes, as well as income taxes”
Moore v. United States, 602 U.S. ___ (2024)).

And since excise tax and thus an income tax is a tax on a privilege, as seen in Flint v. Stone Tracy Co., 220 U.S. 107 (1911):

"As was said in the Thomas case, 192 U. S. 363, supra, the requirement to pay [excise] taxes involves the exercise of privileges...",

it cannot also be a tax on a right, which would be a direct tax,

"Direct taxes bear immediately upon persons, upon the possession and enjoyments of rights;...” (Knowlton v. Moore, 178 U.S. 41 (1900))

That a man’s labor is his most sacred property and unalienable right is also expressed in Butcher’s Union Co. v. Crescent City Co., 111 U.S. 746 (1883),

“[T]he property which every man has in his own labor, as it is the original foundation of all other property, so it is the most sacred and inviolable. The patrimony of the poor man lies in the strength and dexterity of his own hands, and to hinder his employing this strength and dexterity in what manner he thinks proper, without injury to his neighbor, is a plain violation of this most sacred property.” quote from Smith, Wealth of Nations, Bk. I, c. 10.

and in Coppage v. Kansas, 236 U.S. 1 (1915),

“Included in the right of personal liberty and the right of private property, partaking of the nature of each, is the right to make contracts for the acquisition of property, chief among which is that of personal employment by which labor and other services are exchanged for money or other forms of property.”"

from which the natural conclusion is that earnings from unpriviledged labours cannot be taxed as such,

“Since the right to receive [commonly-defined] income or earnings is a right belonging to every person, this right cannot be taxed as privilege.” Jack Cole Company v. MacFarland (337 S.W.2d 453 (1960))

Sims v. On “includes”:

“It is axiomatic that the statutory definition of the term excludes unstated meanings of that term.” Meese v. Keene (1987)

“Meaning of terms: The terms “Includes and including” do not exclude things not enumerated which are in the same general class.” 26 CFR 170.59 [Old and since deleted reference, still used in CFR 27, 72.11]

“[T]he verb “includes” imports a general class, some of whose particular instances are those specified in the definition.” Helvering v Morgan’s, Inc, 293 U.S. 121, 126 fn. 1 (1934)

“[I]ncluding... ...connotes simply an illustrative application of the general principle." Federal Land Bank of St. Paul v. Bismarck Lumber Co., 314 U.S. 95, 62 S.Ct. 1 U.S. (1941).

“When a statute includes an explicit definition, we must follow that definition, even if it varies from that term's ordinary meaning.” Stenberg v. Carhart, 530 U.S. 914 (2000);

“Of course, statutory definitions of terms used therein prevail over colloquial meanings.” Fox v. Standard Oil Co., 294 U.S. 87, 95, 55 S.Ct. 333, 336. Western Union Telegraph Co. v. Lenroot, 323 U.S. 490 (1945);

“[W]e are not at liberty to put our gloss on the definition that Congress provided by looking to the generally accepted meaning of the defined term.” Tenn. Prot. & Advocacy Inc. v. Wells, 371 F.3d 342 (6th Cir. 2004).

“[T]he terms 'includes' and 'including' . . . shall not be deemed to exclude other things otherwise within the meaning of the term defined." 26 U.S.C. § 7701(c). In light of this we apply the principle that a list of terms should be construed to include by implication those additional terms of like kind and class as the expressly included terms. This follows from the canon noscitur a sociis, "a word is known by the company it keeps." Neal v. Clark, 95 U.S. 704, 708-09 (1878).” Brigham v. United States, 160 F.3d 759 (1st Cir. 1998)

“The court also considered that the word "including" was used as a word of enlargement, the learned court being of opinion that such was its ordinary sense. With this we cannot concur.” Montello Salt Co. v. Utah, 221 U.S. 452 (1911)

"[W]here Congress includes particular language in one section of a statute but omits it in another section of the same Act, it is generally presumed that Congress acts intentionally and purposely in the disparate inclusion or exclusion." Russello v. United States, 464 U.S. 16 (1983)

A fundamental rule of statutory construction is that laws which are in pari materia (related in subject matter) are to be read and construed together. “[A]ll acts in pari materia are to be taken together, as if they were one law.” See United States v. Stewart, 311 U.S. 60, 64 (1940), Sanford’s Estate v. Commissioner of Internal Revenue, 308 U.S. 39, 44 (1939), and Harrington v. United States, 78 U.S. 356, 365 (1877)

See also the maxim: “expressio unius est exclusio alterius” or “the expression of one thing is the exclusion of another”.

"In construing federal revenue statute, Supreme Court gives no weight to Treasury regulation which attempts to add to statute something which is not there." United States v. Calamaro, 354 U.S. 351 (1957), 1 L. Ed. 2d 1394, 77 S. Ct. 1138 (1957)

    • “The OTA reemphasized that the argument was frivolous and rejected it ... The Appellant’s arguments, including references to the Classification Act of 1923 influencing Section 61, have been ‘consistently and emphatically rejected by the Office of Tax Appeals, State Board of Equalization, the Appellate Courts, the IRS, who have all found the arguments to be frivolous and without any merit.’”

    • The Franchise Tax Board representative stated: “The Appellant’s attempt to substitute FTB Form 3525 for her W-2 to reflect zero wages is ‘based on position identified and rejected as frivolous,’ with the term ‘employee and wages’ applying to all employees unless specifically exempted by the Internal Revenue Code.”
